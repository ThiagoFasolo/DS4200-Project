Hedge Fund Strategy Performance Analysis

Group Members: Samuel Gioffre, Thiago Fasolo, Nichoals Sparrow

Project Introduction

Hedge funds play a pivotal role in global financial markets by pursuing diverse investment strategies designed to generate risk-adjusted returns independent of traditional benchmarks. Yet the industry’s limited transparency makes it difficult for investors, regulators, and academics to compare performance across styles, regions, and time horizons.

Within this project, our team provides an in-depth analysis of hedge fund performance by examining relationships between risk, return, assets under management (AUM), investment strategies, and key fund characteristics. Using a proprietary dataset from With Intelligence, we explore patterns in volatility, cross-strategy correlations, style persistence, and survivorship effects.

Through interactive visualizations and clear explanations, this website walks through the full analytical process—from data cleaning to final conclusions. Our goal is to make complex financial information more interpretable and to help potential investors identify risk–return profiles that align with their needs, while also offering broader insights into how alternative asset managers behave under different market conditions.

Data Description

We combined two proprietary industry datasets from: With Intelligence

Our dataset contains approximately 8,000 hedge funds with monthly returns from September 2024 - September 2025. For each fund, we have attributes such as primary strategy, country, AUM, and derived metrics including annualized return, volatility, Sharpe ratio, and drawdown.

We used the fund performance dataset containing detailed return metrics, risk measures, and risk data, and the fund profile dataset containing fund metadata including manager information, investment strategies, and geographic locations.

Our data cleaning process followed a structured workflow to ensure the final dataset was consistent, accurate, and fully analysis-ready. We began by selecting only the essential profile and return columns, filtering each dataset to keep the fields actually present and prevent KeyErrors. After subsetting and copying these fields into clean DataFrames, we performed an inner merge on Fund ID to retain only funds appearing in both sources with complete core information. We then standardized data types by converting Inception Date to datetime, stripping percentage signs and commas from all return-related fields, and converting them into numeric values. Fund ID was cast to a string for consistency across joins, while descriptive fields such as fund name, structure, strategies, and geographic information were stored as categorical variables to streamline filtering and improve efficiency. Finally, we removed all rows containing missing values to ensure that only fully complete and reliable fund records remained. This end-to-end process produced a clean, unified dataset suitable for accurate modeling, comparison, and visualization.

References

Hedge Fund Returns and Uncertainty — Timothy Krause

This paper shows that hedge fund returns are strongly linked to market uncertainty using the VVIX index as a proxy. Krause finds that funds most sensitive to VVIX outperform the least sensitive by roughly 5.97% annually—informing our analysis of whether more volatile funds in our dataset similarly outperform less volatile ones.

Measuring Risk in the Hedge Fund Sector — Tobias Adrian

This study highlights how hedge fund risk is driven by volatility and cross-fund covariances. Adrian’s analysis of market conditions surrounding the 1998 LTCM crisis demonstrates that sector-wide co-movement and volatility spikes are central to understanding systemic hedge fund risk.
