Data Cleaning

Our data cleaning process followed a structured workflow to ensure the final dataset was consistent, accurate, and fully analysis-ready. We began by selecting only the essential profile and return columns, filtering each dataset to keep the fields actually present and prevent KeyErrors. After subsetting and copying these fields into clean DataFrames, we performed an inner merge on Fund ID to retain only funds appearing in both sources with complete core information. We then standardized data types by converting Inception Date to datetime, stripping percentage signs and commas from all return-related fields, and converting them into numeric values. Fund ID was cast to a string for consistency across joins, while descriptive fields such as fund name, structure, strategies, and geographic information were stored as categorical variables to streamline filtering and improve efficiency. Finally, we removed all rows containing missing values to ensure that only fully complete and reliable fund records remained. This end-to-end process produced a clean, unified dataset suitable for accurate modeling, comparison, and visualization.

This paper by Timothy Krause showing that hedge fund returns are linked to market uncertainty using the VVIX index (volatility) as a simple proxy. Funds most sensitive to VVIX outperform the least sensitive by ~5.97% per year. We may be able to similarly investigate the most volatile funds and whether they outperform less volatile funds.

Measuring Risk in the Hedge Fund Sector By: Tobias Adrian

This paper shows that hedge fund returns are closely tied to volatility and to covariances across funds, and compares correlations/volatilities around the 1998 Long Term Capital Management LP crisis with equity-market behavior before the crisis. The takeaway is that sector-wide co-movement and volatility are key to understanding hedge fund risk.
