Risk & Return Analysis

Understanding the relationship between risk and return is fundamental to investment analysis. This page explores how different hedge fund strategies position themselves along the risk-return spectrum and evaluates their risk-adjusted performance using the Sharpe ratio. These visualizations help answer critical questions about whether higher risk leads to higher returns and which strategies deliver the best risk-adjusted performance.

Chart 1: Risk vs. Return by Fund

This scatter plot visualizes the relationship between risk (annualized volatility) and return (annualized return) for individual hedge funds, colored by their investment strategy. Each point represents a fund, showing how different strategies position themselves along the risk-return spectrum.

Key Question: Do higher-risk funds deliver higher returns, and which strategies achieve the most efficient risk-return tradeoffs?

Key Insights

Funds show a wide range of risk-return tradeoffs, with some strategies delivering higher returns without taking proportionally more risk. Strategies like Relative Value and Multi-Strategy tend to cluster toward more efficient outcomes.

Chart 2: Sharpe Ratio by Strategy

This box plot displays the distribution of Sharpe Ratios across different hedge fund strategies. The Sharpe Ratio measures risk-adjusted performance, showing how much excess return investors receive per unit of risk taken.

Key Question: Which strategies consistently deliver the best risk-adjusted returns?

Key Insights

Sharpe Ratios vary meaningfully across strategies, indicating large differences in risk-adjusted performance. A few strategies consistently outperform, while others show much lower and more volatile results.

Chart 3: Average Monthly Return by Strategy

This heatmap shows the average monthly returns for each hedge fund strategy over time. The color intensity represents the magnitude of returns, allowing us to identify patterns of performance consistency and volatility across different market conditions.

Key Question: How do strategies perform month-to-month, and are there periods when multiple strategies move together?

Key Insights

Monthly returns exhibit clear clusters where multiple strategies perform well or poorly at the same time, suggesting exposure to shared market conditions. Some strategies show more stable month-to-month behavior than others.

Chart 4: Fund Size vs. Sharpe Ratio

This scatter plot examines the relationship between fund size (assets under management) and risk-adjusted performance (Sharpe Ratio). It helps determine whether larger funds have an advantage in delivering better risk-adjusted returns.

Key Question: Does fund size correlate with better risk-adjusted performance?

Key Insights

There is only a weak relationship between fund size and Sharpe Ratio: Larger funds do not reliably deliver stronger performance. This suggests that scale alone does not determine risk-adjusted results.

This histogram displays the distribution of annualized returns across all hedge funds in the dataset. It reveals the spread of performance outcomes and helps identify the typical range of returns investors can expect.

The distribution of returns is skewed, with many funds clustered near modest positive performance and a tail of both outperformers and underperformers. The gap between mean and median highlights the influence of a few high-return funds.

Understanding the Sharpe Ratio

What is the Sharpe Ratio?

The Sharpe Ratio is calculated as: (Fund Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Standard Deviation

It measures how much excess return an investor receives for the extra volatility they endure by holding a risky asset. A higher Sharpe ratio indicates better risk-adjusted performance. Generally:

  • Below 1.0 - Suboptimal risk-adjusted returns
  • 1.0 to 2.0 - Good risk-adjusted returns
  • Above 2.0 - Excellent risk-adjusted returns
  • Above 3.0 - Exceptional risk-adjusted returns (rare)
